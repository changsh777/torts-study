Class 1 — Intentional Torts (의도적 불법행위)
Cl1-Q1AssaultC
Johnson points unloaded revolver at Curry. Curry knows it's unloaded + ammo locked 2 floors below. Curry laughs.
I: Whether Johnson committed assault against Curry.
R: Assault = intentional act + plaintiff experiences imminent apprehension of harmful/offensive contact. Apprehension must be reasonable.
A: Johnson had intent to frighten. BUT Curry knew gun was unloaded → no imminent apprehension. Unreasonable to fear unloaded gun with ammo locked away.
C: No assault. Curry does not prevail.
Cl1-Q2Battery · Dual IntentC
Passenger lightly taps surgery-recovering woman on arm to return forgotten package. Tap causes painful back injury. Dual-intent jurisdiction.
I: Whether passenger committed battery in a dual-intent state.
R: Dual-intent: defendant must intend (1) the contact AND (2) the harm. Single-intent: intent to contact suffices.
A: Passenger intended the tap (contact ✓). However, he intended to help, not harm (harm intent ✗). In dual-intent state, both required → fails.
C: No battery in dual-intent jurisdiction. Woman does not prevail.
Cl1-Q3Battery · Transferred IntentD
John hits Sam's head with loaded gun. On backswing, gun fires into Sam's stomach. Sam claims battery.
I: Whether Sam can establish battery intent under single-intent and dual-intent rules.
R: Single-intent: intent to contact. Dual-intent: intent to contact + harm. Transferred intent: intent for one act transfers to unintended result.
A: Single: hitting with loaded gun = substantially certain to cause battery ✓. Dual: hitting head = intent to harm; transferred intent carries to stomach shot ✓. Sam prevails under both.
C: Sam establishes intent under both rules.
Class 2 — Property Torts + Defenses (재산 불법행위 + 방어)
Cl2-Q1Trespass to LandA
A forcibly carries B onto C's land against B's will.
I: Who committed trespass to C's land — A, B, or both?
R: Trespass to land = intentional + volitional physical entry onto another's land. The entrant must act voluntarily.
A: B's entry was not volitional — B was carried against his will. A's act and intent caused entry. Only A had the volitional, intentional act.
C: Only A is liable for trespass to C's land.
Cl2-Q2ConversionB
A mistakenly takes B's hat. In (A) returns immediately; (B) returns 3 months later; (C) returns before leaving; (D) keeps his own hat.
I: Which scenario constitutes conversion of B's hat?
R: Conversion = intentional exercise of dominion/control over another's chattel that seriously interferes with possessory rights. Duration/extent of interference matters. Trespass to chattel = minor interference (damages only); conversion = major (full value).
A: (A)(C): brief possession, minimal interference. (D): own hat, no conversion. (B): 3 months = serious, substantial interference → conversion.
C: Only (B) constitutes conversion.
Cl2-Q3Trespass to ChattelD
Person sends malware to company computers.
I: When is sender liable for trespass to chattel?
R: Trespass to chattel = intentional interference with another's right of possession of chattel. Actual damage NOT required — intermeddling itself suffices.
A: Reputation damage (A) ≠ chattel interference. Personal file damage (B) = consequential, not chattel. Disruption (C) = temporary, debatable. Intermeddling with software/hardware (D) = direct interference with chattel ✓.
C: Liable if and only if malware intermeddles with software or hardware.
Cl2-Q4Duress · ConsentA
Defendant threatens (A) plaintiff's child with physical harm; (B) refuses taxes; (C) breakup threat; (D) no donation threat — all to compel sexual activity.
I: Which scenario constitutes duress vitiating consent?
R: Duress = constraint of will by force/threats against person, family member, or valuable property compelling unwilling consent. Threats must be serious and imminent.
A: (B)(C)(D): social/economic pressure ≠ duress. (A): threat of physical harm to child = classic duress ✓.
C: Only (A) constitutes duress. Consent in (A) is vitiated.
Cl2-Q5Defense of Property · Deadly ForceB
Katko v. Briney: defendants use spring shotgun to protect unoccupied farmhouse. Plaintiff-trespasser shot and injured.
I: Whether defendants are liable for using a spring shotgun to protect property.
R: Reasonable force may be used to protect property. However, deadly force may NEVER be used solely to protect property (only to protect persons from imminent death/serious harm).
A: Spring shotgun = deadly force. Property protection alone cannot justify deadly force. No one was present to protect from personal harm → force disproportionate.
C: Defendants liable. Force was unreasonable/deadly for mere property protection.
Cl2-Q6Private NecessityB
Vincent v. Lake Erie: ship captain ties vessel to plaintiff's dock during storm to prevent sinking. Dock damaged.
I: Whether defendant is liable for dock damage caused by private necessity.
R: Private necessity = privilege to interfere with another's property to prevent greater harm. Privilege = no trespass liability. BUT defendant must still pay compensation for actual damage caused.
A: Tying vessel was privileged (private necessity ✓). However, privilege does not negate obligation to compensate for property damage actually caused. "Act of God" defense fails — captain made active choice to stay.
C: Defendant is privileged but must compensate for dock damage.
Class 3 — Negligence Standard of Care (주의의무 기준)
Cl3-Q1Reasonable Person StandardB
2nd-year med student performs Heimlich on choking patron in restaurant. Breaks patron's ribs.
I: What standard of care applies to a medical student who voluntarily assists?
R: Reasonable person standard applies to all. Professionals held to professional standard. Med students = not yet professionals; but training elevates standard above layperson. Standard = reasonable person with similar medical training in similar circumstances.
A: Not a doctor → full physician standard doesn't apply. But medical training → higher than random diner. Court applies: reasonable person with some medical training trying to help choking victim.
C: Standard = reasonable person with similar medical training (B).
Cl3-Q2Negligence per seC
Car owner washes car during rush hour (violates ordinance "to expedite traffic flow"). Wastewater freezes, pedestrian slips and fractures leg.
I: Does violating the ordinance establish negligence per se for the pedestrian's injury?
R: Negligence per se requires: (1) statute violated; (2) plaintiff in protected class; (3) harm is type statute intended to prevent. All three required.
A: Ordinance purpose = expedite traffic flow (not prevent slip-and-fall injuries). Pedestrian ≠ within protected class of automobile operators. Type of harm (slip/fall) ≠ type ordinance prevents. Per se fails → pedestrian's only evidence (statutory violation) insufficient.
C: Grant car owner's motion. No negligence per se. Pedestrian failed to prove breach.
Cl3-Q3Hand FormulaA
Which statement correctly describes the Hand Formula for breach?
I: What does the Hand Formula measure in negligence?
R: Hand Formula (B<PL): Breach occurs when burden of precaution (B) < Probability of harm (P) × Gravity of harm (L). Used to define standard of care (breach), NOT intent or proximate cause.
A: (A) correctly states: P×G > burden → breach. (B) inverts the formula. (C)(D) misapply to intent/causation.
C: A is correct. Hand Formula defines breach when P×L exceeds burden of precaution.
Class 4 — Causation (인과관계)
Cl4-Q1Emergency Doctrine · NegligenceC
Charlie (store clerk) rushes to save child on escalator. Accidentally bumps Helen, who falls and breaks wrist. Helen sues Charlie for negligence.
I: Whether Charlie breached his duty of care to Helen under the emergency doctrine.
R: Emergency doctrine: a person confronted with a sudden, unforeseeable emergency not of their own making is held to a lower standard — what a reasonable person would do in that emergency.
A: Child on escalator = emergency Charlie did not create. Charlie acted to prevent imminent serious harm. A reasonable person in that emergency might also rush without noticing Helen. Conduct was reasonable under emergency standard.
C: Helen does not recover. Charlie reacted reasonably to an emergency not of his making.
Cl4-Q2Causation · But-For TestD
Which causation test do courts use most to establish whether defendant's breach caused plaintiff's harm?
I: What is the primary test for actual causation (cause-in-fact) in negligence?
R: But-for test (majority): plaintiff's injury would not have occurred but for defendant's breach. Substantial factor test: used when multiple causes each independently sufficient. Hand Formula: defines breach, not causation.
A: But-for is default and most commonly used. Substantial factor = exception for concurrent independent causes. Post hoc ergo propter hoc = logical fallacy, not a legal test.
C: But-for test (D) is the primary causation test.
Cl4-Q3Alternative CausationB
Hunters A and B both shoot simultaneously. One bullet hits C. Unknown which gun fired the injuring bullet.
I: What happens when C cannot identify which hunter's shot caused the injury?
R: Alternative-causes doctrine: burden of proof shifts to defendants when (1) two or more negligent defendants; (2) only one could have caused harm; (3) plaintiff can't identify which one. Each defendant must prove they did NOT cause injury. Concert theory: if joint enterprise, both jointly and severally liable.
A: If A proves his bullet did not hit C (meets shifted burden), A is not liable. If A cannot prove this → remains liable. C cannot recover double compensation (D is wrong).
C: If A proves his shot did not injure C, A cannot be held liable (B is true).
Class 5 — Comparative Negligence (비교과실)
Cl5-Q1Modified Comparative NegligenceD ($0)
Fred 55% at fault, Sheila 45% at fault. Total damages $100,000. Modified comparative negligence state. Fred sues Sheila.
I: How much can Fred recover from Sheila in a modified comparative negligence state?
R: Modified comparative negligence: plaintiff's damages reduced by fault percentage. BUT if plaintiff's fault > defendant's (or ≥ 50%, depending on jurisdiction), plaintiff is completely barred.
A: Fred = 55% at fault > Sheila's 45%. Fred's fault exceeds defendant's → complete bar to recovery.
C: Fred recovers $0 from Sheila under modified comparative negligence.
Cl5-Q2Pure Comparative NegligenceC ($45k)
Same facts. Fred 55%, Sheila 45%, $100,000 total. Pure comparative negligence state. Fred sues Sheila.
I: How much can Fred recover under pure comparative negligence?
R: Pure comparative negligence: plaintiff always recovers, but recovery reduced by own fault percentage. No bar regardless of how large plaintiff's fault.
A: Fred is 55% at fault → his damages reduced by 55%. Sheila is 45% at fault → Fred recovers Sheila's 45% = $45,000. No bar even though Fred > 50%.
C: Fred recovers $45,000 (Sheila's 45% of $100,000).
Cl5-Q3Counterclaim · ModifiedB ($55k)
Same facts. Sheila counterclaims against Fred in modified comparative negligence state.
I: How much can Sheila recover from Fred on counterclaim under modified comparative negligence?
R: Modified comparative negligence: plaintiff recovers unless fault > defendant's. Damages reduced by plaintiff's fault percentage.
A: Sheila = 45% fault < Fred's 55% → Sheila is NOT barred. Sheila recovers Fred's fault proportion = 55% of $100,000 = $55,000.
C: Sheila recovers $55,000 from Fred on counterclaim.
Class 6 — Strict Liability + Products Liability (엄격책임 + 제조물책임)
Cl6-Q1Strict Liability · Wild AnimalB
Homeowner keeps defanged poisonous snake in cage. Storm damages cage, snake escapes. Volunteer worker flees, falls, breaks arm.
I: Is homeowner strictly liable even though snake was defanged and cage was damaged by storm?
R: Strict liability for wild animals: owner liable for physical harm caused by wild animal. Extends to reasonably foreseeable reactions (panic, flight) caused by encountering wild animal. No negligence required. Defanging or caging does not negate liability.
A: Snake = wild animal → strict liability. Worker's flight reaction is foreseeable response to free snake. Storm defense (force of nature) fails — strict liability has NO force of nature defense. Defanging irrelevant.
C: Worker prevails. Homeowner strictly liable for foreseeable flight reaction.
Cl6-Q2Products Liability · Failure to WarnB
Tylenol warns about 3+ drinks/day. Helen discards box. Gives bottle to Gwen who drinks 5+/day. Gwen suffers permanent liver damage. Sues Ritegreens.
I: What is Gwen's most promising products liability theory against Ritegreens?
R: Products liability: (1) manufacturing defect (departure from design), (2) design defect (unreasonably dangerous as designed), (3) failure to warn (inadequate warning of known risk). Plaintiff must show defect + causation + injury.
A: No specific manufacturing defect shown. Design defect: acetaminophen's liver risk cannot be designed away (irreducibly unsafe for some) → no RAD. Best theory: warning on bottle ("see box instructions") was inadequate — Gwen never had access to box warning. Failure to warn ✓.
C: Most promising theory = failure to warn (inadequate labeling on bottle itself).
Cl6-Q3Products Liability · RetailerD
ATV dealer Jenner's sells to David. David sells to Carlos. Carlos's throttle defect causes crash injuring bystander Annie. Annie sues Jenner's.
I: Is Jenner's (retailer) strictly liable to Annie (non-purchaser)?
R: Strict products liability: no privity required. Entire distribution chain (manufacturer, distributor, retailer) is liable. Defect must exist when product left defendant's control. Knowledge of defect irrelevant.
A: Privity defense (A) fails — no privity required. Knowledge defense (B) fails — strict liability. Inspection (C) = negligence theory, not strict. If defect existed when Jenner's sold ATV → Jenner's liable as seller in distribution chain.
C: Jenner's liable if defect existed when ATV was sold to David (D).
Class 7 — Nuisance (방해행위)
Cl7-Q1Public Nuisance · StandingA
Acme plant emissions block public view of hills. Carole bought house specifically for that view. Wants injunction.
I: Can Carole bring a private public nuisance action against Acme?
R: Public nuisance = unreasonable interference with right common to general public. State/government asserts public nuisance. Private citizen may only assert public nuisance if harm is DIFFERENT IN KIND from general public's harm.
A: Carole's harm (blocked view) = same type as all other residents. Not different in kind → no private standing for public nuisance. Private nuisance (D) fails — interference is with public view, not her land use specifically. Best option: convince city to file public nuisance claim.
C: Carole's best course = convince city government to file (A). She lacks individual standing.
Cl7-Q2Private NuisanceC
Tom keeps beehives 20 years. Robin builds next door. Bees invade Robin's property, sting him and guests. Tom ignores complaints.
I: Is Tom liable for private nuisance to Robin?
R: Private nuisance = substantial + unreasonable interference with another's use/enjoyment of land. "Coming to the nuisance" (Robin moved there after bees) is NOT a defense.
A: Bees regularly invading Robin's property, stinging people = substantial interference ✓. Unreasonable to allow ongoing stinging despite complaints ✓. Robin bought later → irrelevant (coming to nuisance = not a defense). Tom's lack of intent irrelevant — nuisance = strict interference.
C: Tom liable for private nuisance. Coming to the nuisance is no defense.
Cl7-Q3Public Nuisance · Private Citizen StandingC
Dayton Tower wall collapses onto Main Street. Street closed 3 weeks. Ben's deli loses business (half block away). Ben sues Power Corp. for public nuisance.
I: Can Ben bring a public nuisance claim for economic harm to his deli?
R: Public nuisance: private citizen needs harm "different in kind" (not just degree) from general public. All businesses on Main Street suffered economic loss from closure. Remedies include damages and injunction (D is wrong — both available).
A: Ben's harm (business loss from street closure) = same TYPE as every other business on Main Street. Not different in kind → no standing. Negligence claim might survive, but public nuisance fails.
C: Power Corp. not liable on public nuisance — Ben's harm not different in kind (C).
Class 8 — Privacy Torts (사생활 침해)
Cl8-Q1Public Disclosure · Public FigureC
Times newspaper truthfully reports church leader John's true parentage (result of affair). John sues for public disclosure of private facts.
I: Can John succeed on public disclosure of private facts against the Times?
R: Public disclosure: (1) publicizes; (2) private facts; (3) highly offensive to reasonable person. Defense: matter of legitimate public interest → plaintiff must prove actual malice.
A: John = high-ranking church leader = public figure. His upbringing/parentage = legitimate public concern. Times reported truthfully → no actual malice. John cannot prove actual malice.
C: John unlikely to prevail. Matter of legitimate public concern; no actual malice shown.
Cl8-Q2Intrusion · Public DisclosureB
Prominent businessperson Susie photographed crying next to police car (brother being arrested). Photo published with "sister of alleged drug dealer" caption.
I: Is newspaper liable for intrusion upon seclusion or public disclosure of private facts?
R: Intrusion: intentional intrusion upon plaintiff's solitude that is highly offensive. Exception: photographing in PUBLIC place = not intrusion. Public disclosure: publicizes private facts that are highly offensive. Defense: legitimate public interest + no actual malice.
A: Intrusion: Susie was on public street (police car) → no privacy in public place. Not liable. Disclosure: Susie = prominent businessperson (public figure). Drug connection = public interest. Accurate report → no actual malice. Not liable.
C: Newspaper liable for neither intrusion nor public disclosure (B).
Cl8-Q3Appropriation · False LightA
Joanna (council candidate) uses NBA star James's photos in campaign materials with "Winners Support Winners." James actually supports her opponent. Joanna knows this.
I: Is Joanna liable for appropriation and/or false light?
R: Appropriation: uses plaintiff's name/likeness without consent for defendant's purpose (commercial or otherwise). False light: publicizes false information about plaintiff that is highly offensive to reasonable person.
A: Appropriation: Joanna used James's likeness without consent for political advantage ✓. False light: photos imply James supports Joanna when he supports opponent = false portrayal ✓. Highly offensive = attributed political endorsement one doesn't hold ✓.
C: Joanna liable for BOTH appropriation and false light (A).
Practice — Q1~Q20
P-Q1Battery · Strict Liability · Deadly ForceC
Tom buries explosive in driveway with "No Solicitors / Trespassers will be maimed" sign. CD salesman Rocker ignores sign, drives in, and is injured by explosion.
I: Whether Rocker can recover against Tom for the explosive injury.
R: Battery: intentional act + harmful contact. Substantially certain someone will be injured = intent. No deadly force to protect property. Strict liability: abnormally dangerous activity (explosive) → strictly liable for harm from that danger.
A: Tom planted explosive → substantially certain of harm = battery intent. Defense of property with deadly force = never valid for property alone. Warning sign does not negate liability. Strict liability also applies: explosives = abnormally dangerous. Rocker prevails if Tom planted the charge (C).
C: Rocker prevails if Tom planted the explosive charge.
P-Q2Duty to Rescue · No DutyB/C
Fisher grabs Owens's phone for 10 minutes preventing 911 call while Vick is beaten. Vick dies. Jones = attacker, Owens = bystander, Fisher = bar owner who blocked call.
I: Is Jones liable for battery? Is Owens/Fisher liable for failure to rescue Vick?
R: No general duty to rescue. Exceptions: (1) defendant created peril, (2) special relationship, (3) plaintiff reasonably relied on defendant's promise of rescue. Jones consented to fight → Jones has consent defense to Vick's battery claim.
A: Owens: did not create peril, no special relationship, no promise relied on → no duty. Fisher: took Owens's phone = created obstacle to rescue → could be liable. Jones: consented to fight → valid defense (B/C same answer).
C: Jones not liable (consent defense). Owens not liable (no duty). Fisher may be liable (created rescue obstacle).
P-Q3Informed Consent · CausationB
Doctor fails to disclose 10% blindness risk from brain tumor surgery. Patient consents, goes blind in one eye. Sues for malpractice (negligence).
I: Should Patient prevail on informed consent negligence claim?
R: Informed consent: doctor must disclose material risks. Causation test: would a reasonable patient have refused surgery if informed? Non-disclosure is cause only if reasonable person would not have consented.
A: Breach: 10% blindness risk = material, not disclosed ✓. Causation: key question — if reasonable person in Patient's position would have refused surgery given the risk, non-disclosure = actual cause of blindness. (A) benefits test = wrong standard; (D) intent not required.
C: Patient prevails only if reasonable person would NOT have consented if informed of the risk (B).
P-Q4Contributory Negligence · Professional StandardA
Safety inspector Zilch visits Whizbang plant without hard hat. Part falls from overhead track, causes concussion. Contributory negligence jurisdiction.
I: Is Zilch barred from recovery by contributory negligence?
R: Contributory negligence (majority): any negligence by plaintiff bars recovery entirely. Professional standard: held to standard of reasonable professional in their field. Safety inspector = held to safety inspector standard.
A: Zilch, as professional safety inspector, should know to wear hard hat. Not wearing hard hat = breach of own duty of care. His negligence contributed to injury. In contributory negligence jurisdiction → complete bar.
C: Whizbang prevails. Zilch, given specialized training, should have known the danger. Contributory negligence bars recovery.
P-Q5Contributory Negligence · Intervening ActC
Mario hits Shirley's car → head injury. Later, Shirley goes rollerblading (unwise given balance issues from inner ear damage), falls, breaks hip (osteoporosis). Contributory negligence state.
I: Can Shirley recover for both the head injury and broken hip?
R: Proximate cause: defendant liable for foreseeable consequences. Contributory negligence: plaintiff's own negligence bars recovery for that injury. Eggshell plaintiff: applies to head injury. Rollerblading decision = plaintiff's independent intervening conduct.
A: Head injury: directly caused by Mario ✓. Hip: if rollerblading was unreasonable given balance problems from Mario's accident → Shirley contributorily negligent for hip → bars recovery for hip in contributory negligence state.
C: Head injury only, IF Shirley acted unreasonably by going rollerblading.
P-Q6Child Standard · Alternative CausesB
Rocky (11.5) and Mike (12) throw footballs near roller coaster at Disney. Ball hits visitor in eye. Both children sued.
I: Under what standard of care are both Rocky and Mike liable?
R: Child standard of care: reasonable child of similar age, education, intelligence, and experience. NOT adult standard (exception: child engaging in adult activities, e.g., driving). Both Rocky and Mike participated in the throwing activity.
A: Adult standard (A) = wrong. Rocky didn't throw (C/D) = wrong; Rocky participated in the activity. Both could be liable if child of similar age/experience wouldn't throw there.
C: Both liable if reasonable child of their age, education, intelligence, experience would not have thrown football there (B).
P-Q7Trespass to Land · Strict LiabilityD
Dakota (12) flies model airplane. It malfunctions, crash-lands on Hank's prize roses. Hank sues.
I: Which fact is MOST helpful to Hank in establishing Dakota's liability?
R: Trespass to land: intentional act causing physical intrusion onto land. Negligence: requires breach of care. Child standard: child of similar attributes. Most helpful = easiest to prove/most favorable theory.
A: (A)(B): negligence theories → require breach analysis. (C): warning = relevant to negligence. (D): trespass to land → no negligence required; Dakota intentionally flew plane → crashed on Hank's land = trespass. Strongest/easiest theory for Hank.
C: Trespass to land (D) is most helpful — no need to prove breach of care.
P-Q8Negligence · Damages at Time of ActD
Dr. Doolittle gives anesthesia to 9-year-old Ricky. Undiscoverable metabolic condition causes no immediate harm but neurological damage manifests at age 30. Ricky (30) sues.
I: Can Ricky recover for neurological damage that manifested 21 years after treatment?
R: Negligence: requires actual damages. Plaintiff must have suffered harm at time of or causally linked to breach. Future harm (if discoverable) may satisfy damages, but harm must be actual, not speculative.
A: At time of treatment: Ricky suffered no harm — anesthesia worked for procedure, no immediate effect. Damage appeared 21 years later. No injury at time of treatment = no negligence claim at that time. Statute of limitations also likely expired.
C: Ricky does not prevail. No harm suffered at time of the negligent act (D).
P-Q9Assault · ApprehensionA
Patience rear-ends Dimitri's car. Dimitri (unaware of danger while seated) gets out and screams at Patience. Patience did not know he was in the car. Dimitri sues for assault.
I: Did Patience commit assault against Dimitri?
R: Assault: defendant's intentional act causes plaintiff's reasonable apprehension of imminent harmful contact. Apprehension must occur during the act. Negligence alone ≠ assault.
A: Patience did not know Dimitri was in the car → could not have intentionally placed him in apprehension. Dimitri was not aware of danger while in car (no apprehension during act). However, AK = A: Patience "did not know at the time he was in danger" → supports Dimitri's claim that there was no valid assault defense. ⚠ Fact pattern logic: Dimitri prevails under A per AK.
C: AK answer = A. Patience did not know Dimitri was in danger from her driving → supports Dimitri winning.
P-Q10Negligence · Duty to WarnB
Martha (seamstress) hires Winifred as model. Winifred has undisclosed skin condition aggravated by certain fabrics. Winifred develops rash. Sues Martha for negligence.
I: Is Martha liable for Winifred's skin condition caused by the fabric?
R: Duty to warn: obligation to warn of known or reasonably knowable dangers. No duty to warn of unknown and unknowable risks. Landowner/employer duty extends to known conditions.
A: Martha did not know and could not reasonably have known of Winifred's skin condition. Martha's duty = warn of known/knowable dangers on her property. Unknown individual medical condition ≠ Martha's obligation to discover. No breach.
C: Martha not liable. Her duty = warn of known/knowable dangers only. Winifred's condition was unknown and unknowable (B).
P-Q11Battery · Consent DefenseD
Sam and Mike play paintball. Sam intentionally shoots Mike in face; Mike loses sight in right eye despite wearing goggles. Mike sues Sam for battery.
I: What is Sam's best defense to Mike's battery claim?
R: Consent = complete defense to battery if plaintiff voluntarily agreed to the contact. Scope: consent extends to foreseeable contact within the agreed activity. Assumed risk (B) ≠ same as consent but related.
A: (A) intent to injure not required for battery. (B) assumption of risk = defense but not specific to battery. (C) actual cause ≠ defense. (D) Mike consented to paintball contact → face shots within scope of game contact ✓. Best defense = consent.
C: Sam's best defense = Mike consented to the contact that occurred during the game (D).
P-Q12Wrongful Death · Survival ActionB
Megan dies on defective roller coaster. Frank (executor/husband) wants damages for loss of her guidance, advice, counsel, and consortium.
I: Which action allows Frank to recover for loss of consortium/guidance?
R: Survival action: decedent's own claims survive (e.g., her pain/suffering before death). Wrongful death: survivors recover for their OWN loss caused by decedent's death (consortium, guidance, financial support).
A: Consortium/guidance = Frank's personal loss = wrongful death theory. (A) survival action = Megan's pain before death, not Frank's loss. (C)(D) earning potential = economic damages, not consortium.
C: Frank should bring a wrongful death action for his own loss of guidance/consortium (B).
P-Q13Products Liability · Warning vs DesignA
"A warning on a product:" — what is true?
I: What is the legal effect of a product warning on design defect analysis?
R: Warning = one factor in risk-utility test for design defect. Warning does NOT cure a design defect — if design is defective, warning alone doesn't fix it. Failure to warn: causation requires plaintiff would have read and heeded the warning.
A: (A) Warning = one factor in design defect analysis ✓. (B)(C) "completely cures" = wrong — warning never cures design defect. (D) If plaintiff didn't read warning → no causation for failure-to-warn claim (D is backwards).
C: Warning is ONE factor in design defect analysis, not a cure (A).
P-Q14Strict Liability — What's NOT Part of ClaimB
Which element would NEVER be part of a strict liability claim?
I: Which of the four options is incompatible with strict liability?
R: Strict liability = liability without fault. Proof of negligence/care is irrelevant. Elements: defect, defect existed when left control, causation, damages. "Should have been more careful" = negligence standard only.
A: (A) Punitive damages = possible in strict liability. (C) Actual damages = required. (D) Defect = required. (B) "Whether defendant should have been more careful" = negligence standard = NEVER relevant in strict liability.
C: "Whether defendant should have been more careful" (B) is never part of strict liability.
P-Q15Defamation · Judge vs JuryB
Newspaper statement has only one reasonable interpretation. Is defamation a question of law or fact?
I: When a statement has only one reasonable interpretation, who decides if it's defamatory?
R: Defamation: whether statement is capable of defamatory meaning = threshold question. If only ONE reasonable interpretation → judge decides as matter of law (no jury needed). If multiple interpretations possible → jury question.
A: One interpretation → unambiguous → court decides as matter of law. (A) "always jury" = wrong. (C) "always judge" = wrong (multiple interpretations → jury). (D) jury first then judge = wrong procedure.
C: Court may find statement defamatory as matter of law when only one reasonable interpretation exists (B).
P-Q16Defamation per seA
Which factor most likely produces a finding of defamation per se?
I: Which category most clearly constitutes defamation per se?
R: Defamation per se: statement defamatory on its face without need to prove special damages. Four categories: (1) crime of moral turpitude, (2) loathsome disease, (3) business/professional misconduct, (4) sexual misconduct. Publication medium and audience size are not per se factors.
A: (A) Accusing of crime = moral turpitude → classic per se category ✓. (B) newspaper vs verbal = affects slander/libel distinction, not per se. (C) public figure = relevant to actual malice, not per se. (D) audience size = damages, not per se.
C: Accusing plaintiff of crime involving moral turpitude (A) = strongest defamation per se factor.
P-Q17Products Liability · No PrivityB
LadderCo makes defective wheel. Sammy buys from dealer → sells to Annie. Annie crashes. Sues manufacturer.
I: Can Annie (second-hand buyer) sue manufacturer for strict products liability?
R: Strict products liability: no privity required. Any foreseeable user/bystander can sue. Defect must exist when product left defendant's control. Knowledge irrelevant.
A: (A) no privity needed. (C) knowledge irrelevant. (D) Sammy being original buyer irrelevant — Annie is foreseeable user. (B) Annie prevails if defect existed when car left manufacturer's control ✓.
C: Annie prevails if wheel was defective when car left manufacturer's control (B).
P-Q18Negligence per se · Lead PaintC
City ordinance requires lead paint removal. Sam (landlord) doesn't remove it. Tenant's child is poisoned. Guardian sues Sam for negligence.
I: Is Sam liable for violating the lead paint removal ordinance?
R: Negligence per se: (1) statute violated; (2) plaintiff in protected class; (3) harm = type statute intended to prevent. All three → breach conclusively established.
A: Sam violated ordinance ✓. Ordinance purpose = prevent lead poisoning in residents ✓. Child = resident/protected class ✓. Harm = lead poisoning = exact harm ordinance was designed to prevent ✓. All three elements met → negligence per se.
C: Sam liable — violated city ordinance designed to prevent this exact type of harm (C).
P-Q19Nuisance — GeneralB
"Nuisances are: (A) usually unintentional (B) usually private (C) usually high-value conduct (D) usually basis for injunction for economic loss"
I: Which statement is generally true about nuisances?
R: Most nuisance cases involve private nuisance (individual land use interference). Public nuisance is less common. Nuisance typically involves intentional conduct (defendant intends the activity). Injunction most common remedy, but not exclusively for economic loss.
A: (A) usually intentional, not unintentional. (C) high-value conduct = utility factor in nuisance balancing, not a defining characteristic. (D) injunction not limited to economic loss. (B) most nuisance cases = private nuisance ✓.
C: Nuisances are usually private (B).
P-Q20Products Liability · Irreducibly UnsafeB
"Which is true about products liability claims?" — re: irreducibly unsafe products, strict liability, privity.
I: Which statement correctly describes products liability law?
R: Design defect requires a reasonable alternative design (RAD). Irreducibly unsafe products (knives, alcohol, guns) have no RAD → cannot form basis for design defect claim. Strict liability ≠ requires negligence. Privity not required.
A: (A) not always strictly liable (defect must be proven). (C) strict liability ≠ negligently made — strict = no fault. (D) privity not required. (B) irreducibly unsafe = no RAD → no design defect claim ✓.
C: Irreducibly unsafe products cannot form basis for design defect claim because no RAD exists (B).
